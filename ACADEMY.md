# Forge Academy
### A School for People Who Want to Build Real Things

**By Sovereign Digital LLC — 13 Moon Forge**

---

## What This School Is

Forge Academy is a two-year coding school built inside the 13 Moon Forge ecosystem. It exists for one reason: to take a person who has never written a line of code and turn them into a developer who can build, ship, and sell real software.

Not a developer who finished a course. Not a developer who can copy tutorials. A developer who looks at a problem, knows how to approach it, builds a solution, puts it on the internet, and charges for it.

That takes two years of real work. This document is honest about what that looks like — month by month, level by level — and what a student can realistically expect to be able to do at each stage.

At the end of the Academy, there is a final project. Not a test. Not a portfolio piece. A real product — the student's own idea, built by the student's own hands, supported by Moon AI and the Academy community, and ready to be sold the day it's finished. That is the graduation requirement and the graduation gift.

---

## Who This Is For

Anyone who can read. There are no other requirements.

- No age limit
- No prior experience
- No technical background
- No computer science degree
- No math beyond basic arithmetic

The Academy accepts complete beginners and people with some experience who want to fill gaps, formalize what they know, and build a real portfolio. The placement assessment places every student at their actual level — no sitting through what you already know, no skipping what you actually need.

## What Every Student Leaves With

Every student who finishes the full Academy curriculum leaves with three things they built themselves:

**1. Their Website**
Started in Level 3 as three lines of HTML. By Level 12, it's a live, professional website at a real domain — designed, functional, and ready to represent them to potential clients or employers. Could be a portfolio, a business site, a store, a blog. The student decides what it is. It's theirs.

**2. Their App**
A real web application — front end, back end, and database — deployed and accessible to anyone in the world. It stores data. It has user accounts. It does something genuinely useful. The student chose what to build. By the time it's done, it's a product that could be subscribed to, charged for, or used to land a client.

**3. Their Game**
A complete, playable video game. Small by commercial standards, but finished — which matters more than size. It has a title screen, a game loop, a way to win or lose, and a score. It ships to itch.io when it's done. The same day the Level 12 capstone is complete, the game is available to millions of people.

Everything listed above is the student's intellectual property. Not Sovereign Digital's. Not the Forge's. The student's. To sell, to show, to keep building, or to start a business around.

---

## The Three Core Principles

**1. No level teaches just one thing.**
Every lesson connects to all three builds — website, app, and game — at the same time. A lesson on JavaScript loops shows how a website displays a list, how an app processes form data, and how a game moves an enemy. Students never wonder "when would I use this?" They're already using it in three places at once.

**2. Everything you build, you own.**
Every project is the student's idea. The Academy provides the skills and the structure — the student provides the vision. By Level 6, the student has chosen what their app will be. By Level 10, they've chosen what their game will be. These aren't assigned exercises. They're real products.

**3. You can always go back and make it better.**
At any level, a student can return to something they built earlier and improve it with what they know now. The website built in Level 3 can get CSS in Level 4, JavaScript in Level 5, a back end in Level 7, a database in Level 8, and a real domain in Level 11. Each time they return, they see how much they've grown. The Forge keeps every version.

## Month 1–2 — Level 1 and Level 2
### Context, History, and How the World Works

Before a student writes a single line of code, they understand the world they're entering.

**Level 1 covers:**

*The history:*
- Ada Lovelace in 1843 — the first person who understood that a machine could follow written instructions to do anything, not just calculate
- Alan Turing — the mathematician who defined what a computer is before computers existed
- Grace Hopper — who wrote the first compiler, coined the term "bug" after pulling a real moth out of a relay, and proved that code didn't have to look like machine instructions
- The inventors of the internet, the web, and the languages — all of them were people solving problems, not mythical geniuses

*The companies — the full industry landscape:*

Every piece of software a student has ever used was built by someone. Level 1 names them.

- **Microsoft** — built Windows and Office, now owns GitHub and VS Code (the most-used code editor on earth), runs Azure (one of the three major clouds), and created TypeScript, the language the Forge is built in
- **Apple** — built the Mac, the iPhone, and iOS. iPhone developers use Swift, a language Apple created. The App Store controls how most mobile software reaches people — and Apple takes 30% of every dollar
- **Google** — built Search, Chrome, Android, YouTube, and Google Cloud. Created Go (a programming language), contributes massively to open source, and owns the most influential AI research team in the world
- **Meta (Facebook)** — built Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp. Created React, the most popular way to build web interfaces in the world — also what the Forge is built with
- **Amazon** — started as a bookstore, became the backbone of the internet. AWS powers a significant portion of every website a person visits. When AWS goes down, half the internet goes with it
- **Epic Games** — built Fortnite and Unreal Engine, one of the two dominant game engines. Unreal is free. Epic has spent years fighting Apple's 30% cut in court on behalf of all developers
- **Unity** — the other major game engine. Powers most indie games. Changed their pricing in 2023 in a way that enraged developers overnight — a real lesson in why business models matter as much as features
- **Valve / Steam** — built the dominant PC gaming marketplace and the Source engine (Half-Life, Portal, Counter-Strike). Run with almost no management structure, one of the most unusual successful companies in tech
- **id Software** — built Doom and Quake, then released the source code for free. John Carmack taught a generation of developers how games work by giving the code away. One of the most important acts in programming history
- **The open source world** — Linux (running most servers on earth, including the Forge's), Mozilla (Firefox), the Apache Foundation, the Linux Foundation. Software nobody owns and everyone uses
- **Indie developers** — Minecraft: one person. Stardew Valley: one person. Dwarf Fortress: two people. The category this Academy exists to grow

*The tools:*
- What a text editor is and why developers use one instead of Word
- What the terminal is and why it still runs the modern world despite looking like 1985
- What a browser's developer tools are and how to open them right now on any website
- The Forge as the workshop — everything needed is already here, nothing to install, nothing to configure

**Level 2 covers:**

*How the computer actually works:*
- What happens between pressing a key and something appearing on screen — the full chain in plain English
- What a file is, what a folder is, what a program is — in real terms, not marketing terms
- What it means to "run" something and what an error actually means
- Why computers do exactly what you tell them, not what you meant — and why that's actually good

*How the web actually works:*
- What happens between typing a URL and seeing a page — every step in the full journey
- What is a browser, what is a server, what is the difference between the two
- What HTML, CSS, and JavaScript are — one honest sentence each, before touching any of them
- Why websites are made of plain text files that any human can open and read

**Where a student is at the end of Month 2:**

They haven't written code yet. That's intentional. They understand the world they're building for, the companies that built the tools they'll use, the history of the craft, and how a website actually gets from a server to a screen. When they write their first line of code in Month 3, they'll understand what it's doing and why.

This context is what separates someone who can code from someone who understands code.

The website has existed since Month 3 as text on a white page. Level 4 changes that.

**What's covered:**

*CSS fundamentals:*
- What CSS is and why HTML and CSS are two different languages doing two different jobs
- Selectors — how CSS knows which element to style
- Colors, fonts, spacing, borders — the basic vocabulary of visual design in code
- The box model — every element on a webpage is a box inside boxes, and understanding this unlocks everything

*Layout:*
- Flexbox — the modern way to lay things out on a page without fighting the browser
- Responsive design — making a page that looks right on a phone AND a computer, using the same code
- Navigation bars, cards, grids — real patterns that real websites use

*Connecting to the bigger picture:*
- How game UI uses CSS concepts — health bars are divs with widths, menus are positioned elements, score displays are styled text

*Design basics for developers:*
- Color theory in 10 minutes — warm/cool, contrast, readability
- Typography choices that don't require a design degree
- Why whitespace is the most underrated tool in design
- The difference between a page that looks built by a developer and a page that looks built by a designer — and how to close that gap

**Website milestone:** By the end of Level 4, the student has a real website. Not three lines of HTML — a real page with a header, navigation, sections, styled text, and a layout that looks intentional. It works on a phone. It could pass for something a professional made.

**Where a student is at the end of Month 4:**

The student has something they can show someone without apologizing for how it looks. This is a real milestone. Most people learning to code go months without having anything they're proud to share. By Month 4 in the Academy, they have a website that looks like something.

## Months 5–6 — Level 5
### JavaScript — Making It Do Things

This is the level where many self-taught developers get stuck and quit. The Academy is built around making sure that doesn't happen — not by making JavaScript easier, but by making it make sense.

**Why two months:**

JavaScript is the first language students learn that has real logic in it. HTML tells a page what to contain. CSS tells it how to look. JavaScript tells it what to *do*. That's a fundamentally different kind of thinking, and it takes time to build.

**What's covered, in order:**

*Variables in JavaScript:*
- The same concept from Level 3, now in the language that makes things happen
- `let`, `const` — what the difference is and why it matters
- Strings, numbers, booleans — the three basic types of information

*Conditions:*
- If/else — the decision that every program ever written is built from
- Comparison operators — greater than, less than, equal to, not equal
- Why `=` means "assign" and `===` means "compare" — the distinction that trips up every beginner

*Loops:*
- The ability to do something many times without writing it many times
- `for` loops — counting through a list
- `while` loops — keep going until something is true

*Functions:*
- How to give a piece of code a name and use it again later
- Parameters — the inputs a function can accept
- Return values — the output a function can give back

*The DOM:*
- What the Document Object Model is and why it's how JavaScript talks to a webpage
- `document.getElementById`, `querySelector` — finding elements on a page
- Changing what's on the page with JavaScript — updating text, showing and hiding things

**Every concept connected to all three builds:**

- Loops in a website: displaying a list of articles, rendering navigation items from an array
- Loops in an app: processing a list of form entries, displaying search results
- Loops in a game: moving all enemies at once, checking all collectibles, rendering a grid

*Students see this explicitly.* The lesson on loops shows all three at the same time.

**Website milestone:** The website responds. A menu opens and closes. A form validates itself before submitting. A button changes something on the page. The website is no longer a static document — it's interactive.

**The honest truth about Month 5–6:**

JavaScript is hard in a way that HTML and CSS are not. There will be errors that don't make sense. There will be moments where it works and the student doesn't know why. There will be frustrating sessions where something simple takes two hours.

This is normal. This is what learning to code actually feels like. The Academy prepares students for this explicitly — not by promising it will be easy, but by teaching them that confusion is information. An error message is the computer trying to tell you what went wrong. Learning to read error messages is one of the most important skills in all of programming.

**Where a student is at the end of Month 6:**

The student can write real JavaScript. They can make a webpage respond to user input. They can write a function, call it from a button click, change what the page shows based on a condition. They can debug something that isn't working by reading the error and reasoning through it. This is the hardest part of Year One, and they've made it through.

## Months 9–10 — Level 7
### Servers and APIs — The Other Half of Everything

Every time a student has ever submitted a form, logged into an account, or seen content that updated without refreshing — a back end made that possible. Level 7 makes the invisible visible.

**What's covered:**

*What a server is:*
- A computer somewhere else that is always running and waiting to receive requests
- How a request travels from a browser to a server and back
- What HTTP is — the protocol that every web request uses, every time, without exception
- The difference between GET (asking for something) and POST (sending something)

*What an API is:*
- Application Programming Interface — a defined way for two pieces of software to talk to each other
- Why APIs exist and why almost every modern app is built on top of several of them
- Making a real API request from the browser and seeing what comes back
- Reading API documentation — the skill that connects a developer to the entire internet

*Building a simple server:*
- Setting up a Node.js server for the first time
- Writing a route — a specific address the server responds to
- Returning real data from a real endpoint
- Connecting the front-end website to the server and seeing data flow between them

**Website milestone:** The website can now send and receive data from a server. A contact form that emails someone. A page that loads content from an API instead of hard-coding it.

**App milestone:** The app now has a real back end. Data doesn't just live in the browser — it lives on a server. Two people on different computers can see the same information.

**Game connection:** Online leaderboards. Save states that survive across devices. Multiplayer (a future project, not a Level 7 project — but students can now see how it would work).

**Where a student is at the end of Month 10:**

The student can build a front end and a back end and make them talk to each other. This is one of the most significant milestones in learning to code — the moment the student stops building toys and starts building systems.

## Months 11–12 — Level 8
### Databases — Where Data Lives

Every account a person has ever created, every post they've ever saved, every score they've ever earned — all of it lives in a database. Level 8 is where students learn to build and use one.

**What's covered:**

*What a database is:*
- Tables, rows, and columns — explained through real examples (users, posts, scores)
- The difference between a database and a file — why you can't just save everything to a text file and call it done
- SQL — the language for talking to a database in plain, readable commands
- Basic operations: SELECT (get data), INSERT (add data), UPDATE (change data), DELETE (remove data)

*Connecting a database to the back end:*
- Setting up a real PostgreSQL database
- Connecting the server to the database
- Writing routes that read from and write to real data
- What happens when a database query fails — and how to handle it gracefully

*Data relationships:*
- How a user connects to their posts
- How a post connects to its comments
- Why data is organized in tables instead of one giant pile

**App milestone:** The student's app now has a real database. Users can create accounts. Their data persists between sessions, across devices, forever — not just in a browser's localStorage. The app is now what most people mean when they say "a real app."

**Website milestone:** The website can now have real content that lives in a database. A blog where posts are stored. A gallery that can be updated without editing code.

**Game connection:** Player profiles stored in a database. A high score table that persists. Saved game state accessible from any device.

**Where a student is at the end of Month 12:**

The student can build and query a real database. They can connect it to a real back end. They understand how data is stored, retrieved, and organized. They're two-thirds of the way through the most technically demanding part of the curriculum and they've made it through both the hardest conceptual shifts.

## Months 15–16 — Level 10
### Git — The Professional's Most Important Tool

Every working developer uses Git. Every one of them. It is the single most universal tool in software development.

**What's covered:**

*What Git is:*
- Version control — a system that tracks every change made to a codebase over time
- Why this matters: going back to a version that worked, understanding what changed, working with other developers without destroying each other's work
- The mental model: commits, branches, history — what each one means and why it exists

*The core workflow:*
- `git init` — starting a repository
- `git add` — staging changes to save
- `git commit` — saving a snapshot of the current state with a message
- `git log` — seeing the history of what changed and when
- `git diff` — seeing exactly what's different between two states
- `git branch` — creating a separate line of work
- `git merge` — combining work from two branches

*GitHub / Forgejo:*
- Pushing code to a remote repository
- The difference between Git (the tool) and GitHub (a hosting service for Git repositories)
- Why a remote repository is insurance — your code exists somewhere even if your computer dies

*Git for all three builds:*
- Every project the student has built goes into a real repository
- Every future project starts in a repository on day one
- The history of each build — from Level 3 to Level 12 — is preserved and readable

**Game build begins:**

The game starts here. In a Git repository. With a README. Managed like a professional software project from the first commit. By the time the game is done in Level 12, it will have a complete, readable history of how it was built.

**Where a student is at the end of Month 16:**

The student uses Git naturally. They commit when they finish something. They branch when they're trying something risky. They read the log when something breaks. Git is no longer a scary thing — it's a habit, like saving a file.

## Months 17–20 — Level 11
### Deployment — The Moment It Becomes Real

Deployment is what separates software that works on one person's computer from software that anyone in the world can use. Level 11 is the level where everything goes live.

**What's covered:**

*What deployment is:*
- Taking code that runs locally and putting it on a server that is always running
- DNS — how a domain name becomes an IP address that points to a server
- HTTPS — why every real website needs it and how it works
- Environment variables — secrets and configuration that live on the server, not in the code

*Deploying a website:*
- Choosing a hosting solution
- Setting up a domain
- Configuring the server to serve the right files
- Making it fast — basic performance practices that make a real difference

*Deploying an app:*
- Deploying a Node.js server
- Connecting the deployed back end to the deployed database
- Making sure the database is backed up
- What happens when something goes wrong in production — and how to find out what

*Deploying a game:*
- itch.io as a publishing platform for indie games
- Packaging the game for the web
- Writing a real store page — description, screenshots, pricing
- The moment the game goes live and becomes available to millions of people

**The three-milestone day:**

Level 11 ends with the student's three builds all live on the internet at the same time:
- Website at a real domain
- App accessible to any real user
- Game available on itch.io

This is the day the student becomes someone who has shipped. Not practiced shipping. Actually shipped. That is the line that separates developers who have something to show from developers who are still getting ready.

**Where a student is at the end of Month 20:**

The student has three live, working products on the internet. They know how to deploy software. They understand DNS, HTTPS, environment variables, and production servers. They have faced a real production issue — because everyone does — and resolved it. They are ready for the capstone.

## Months 21–24 — Level 12
### The Capstone — Finish What You Started or Start Something New

Level 12 is the final level of the Academy curriculum. The capstone is the student's choice.

**The choice:**

Option A — Take one of the three builds and make it the best version it can be. Fully designed. Fully functional. Polished enough to charge for. This is the path for a student who built something in an earlier level that they genuinely believe in and want to take further.

Option B — Start something new. Apply everything from Levels 1 through 11 to a fresh idea. Build it from scratch, from the first commit to the deployed product, in the final months of the curriculum.

Both options produce the same result: a real, deployed, finished project. The student decides which path leads there.

**The game capstone:**

For the game track, the Level 12 capstone is a complete, playable game that includes:
- A title screen
- A core game loop — the thing the player does, over and over, that makes the game a game
- Collision detection — knowing when something hit something else
- A score or win/loss condition
- Sound (optional but encouraged)
- A share link — something the player can send to a friend

The game is published to itch.io. On the day it's finished, it is available to everyone.

**The professional standard:**

By Level 12, a Journeyman's work should meet a professional standard:
- A website built at Level 12 can be sold to a client
- An app built at Level 12 can be subscribed to
- A game built at Level 12 can charge a dollar on itch.io and people will pay it

This isn't aspirational. It's the standard the curriculum is built to reach.

**Completing Level 12 earns Journeyman status.**

Three builds. All live. All theirs. All capable of generating income. The credential is real because the portfolio behind it is real.

# THE MASTER FINAL — The Project That Graduates You

The Academy curriculum ends at Level 12. Master is what comes after — and it has different rules.

Master is not a class you take. It's not a level you level up through. It is a period of real-world work — roughly a year of it — that culminates in one final project: a product the student chose to build, built by the student's own hands, supported by Moon AI and the Academy community, designed to exist in the world and generate real revenue.

This is the graduation requirement. And it is the graduation gift.

---

## What the Master Final Project Is

The Master Final is the student's own idea. Not an assigned topic. Not a category they were given. Something they want to exist in the world and are willing to put a year of serious work into.

It must meet the following:

**1. It must be real.**
Not a tutorial. Not a clone of something else. An original product. It can be inspired by something that exists — most great products are — but it must bring something that only this student could bring to it.

**2. It must be complete.**
Not a prototype. Not an MVP that was abandoned after the first version. A product with:
- A real front end that real people can use
- A real back end that handles real data
- A database that stores real information
- A deployment that anyone in the world can access
- A way to pay for it, if it charges money

**3. It must be sellable.**
The Master Final must either:
- Have at least one paying customer, OR
- Be listed on a marketplace (itch.io, the Forge's Town Square, the App Store, the web) with a real price

The requirement is not that it's profitable. The requirement is that it is ready to be — that the student took it all the way to "someone could pay for this today."

**4. The student must be able to explain all of it.**
Every line of code. Every architectural decision. Every tradeoff made. This is not a memory test — it's a proof that the student built it. When a student can explain how something works, they own it. When they can't, they didn't build it.

## How the Moons Support the Final Project

The student builds the Master Final themselves. But they don't build it alone.

**Moon AI — the technical mentor:**

Moon AI is available at every hour of every day of the build. Not to write code for the student — to think alongside them.

- "I'm trying to build a feature that does X. What's the right approach?"
- "I'm getting this error and I've been stuck for two hours. Help me think through it."
- "I've built two versions of this and I'm not sure which one is better. Walk me through the tradeoffs."
- "I want to add authentication to my app. What should I know before I start?"

Moon AI reviews code, asks questions, points out what could go wrong, and suggests what to read — but it does not write the student's code for them. The final project must be the student's. Moon AI is the mentor who knows everything and is always available, not the co-author who does half the work.

**The Academy community:**

Other Academy students and graduates are part of the support system. Journeymen who finished the curriculum before you, Masters who are deep in their real-world work, people who hit the same problem three months ago and know exactly what caused it. The Forge's Academy community is where students post questions, share progress, and get real feedback from people who are building.

**Sovereign Digital — the final review:**

Before a Master Final is officially accepted as graduation-complete, it goes through a final human review. A real person looks at the project, uses it, reads the code, and has a conversation with the student. The review asks:
- Does it work?
- Did the student build it?
- Can the student explain it?
- Is it ready to sell?
