# COULD IT ACTUALLY WORK
## The Honest Assessment of Whether Council Continuism Could Be Implemented in Real Life

*This document does not cheerlead. The answer is real, and the answer is yes — with specific conditions. The conditions are named honestly here, including the ones that would make it fail.*

---

## The Direct Answer

**Yes. This system could actually work — if it is implemented under the right conditions.**

It can also fail badly if implemented under the wrong ones. The difference between success and failure is not luck. It is whether the founding conditions are honest about what this system requires.

The rest of this document is the honest accounting.

---

## What Already Proves the Foundation Is Real

This is not theoretical. Pieces of this system have been working in real communities — some of them for decades, some of them for centuries. None has all the elements we've designed. But every piece has been demonstrated in practice somewhere.

**The Religious Society of Friends (Quakers).** 350+ years. Consensus-based decision-making with no presiding authority and every voice. The Quaker meeting is the closest historical practice to the first round of the council. They proved that collective decision-making without votes can work consistently across centuries. Limited by Quaker theology being the cultural binding force — a community without that binding needs something else.

**Twin Oaks Community** (Virginia, 1967 to present). 57 years. About 100 people. Income-sharing intentional community using a planner-manager system that is council-adjacent. They proved that a non-hierarchical community of ~100 people can sustain itself materially and institutionally for multiple generations.

**Christiania** (Copenhagen, 1971 to present). 53 years. Autonomous community in the middle of a major European city, operating on consensus-based council without formal leadership. Has survived multiple attempts by the Danish government to dismantle it. Proves that a system like this can survive in the middle of a hostile state.

**The Zapatistas** (Chiapas, Mexico, 1994 to present). 30 years. Tens of thousands of people across multiple villages organized through rotating delegate councils on the principle of *mandar obedeciendo* — "to govern by obeying." Proves that council-based governance can work at significant scale (multiple connected communities), under continuous external pressure from a state actively hostile to its existence, across generations.

**Rojava / AANES** (Northeast Syria, 2012 to present). 12 years. Several million people organized on democratic confederalism — Bookchin's libertarian municipalism scaled up. Council-based, women-co-led, multi-ethnic. Has functioned under continuous war conditions for over a decade. Proves the system can function even under extreme external attack.

**The Iroquois Confederacy** (Haudenosaunee, ~1100s to ~1770s active sovereignty, continuing today). 600+ years. Council-based federation of multiple nations operating on consensus principles, with women selecting council members and having the power to remove them. Influential on the U.S. Constitution — but the influences the founders took were the structural ones, not the consensus or council ones. Proves council-based governance can scale to a multi-nation federation across centuries.

**The takeaway from the historical record:** Every component of this system has been proven somewhere. None of these communities has everything we've designed. **Council Continuism is the first complete design** that integrates what each of them did partially. The pieces work. The integration has not yet been built. The argument is that integration is what closes the gaps that brought each partial implementation to its limits.

## The Conditions Required for It to Work

Honest list. Skip any of these and the implementation is much more likely to fail.

**One — Small to medium scale at founding.**

Thirty to two hundred people. This is the size where the council mechanics work directly, without the nested structure. Above two hundred, the system requires nesting — local councils with inter-council coordination — which adds complexity. Above a few thousand, it stops being the same system and becomes something else (more like Rojava or the Zapatistas, which is also viable but different).

A community that tries to start at scale will fail. Start small. Grow at the speed of demonstrated trust. Twin Oaks intentionally caps at ~100. Zapatistas grew village by village. Get the small version right before scaling.

**Two — Shared physical space.**

The council requires presence. People walking in and out, hot moments where the room fills, children sitting at the edges and then at the table — none of this works distributed. A digital version would lose the energy that makes it work.

This means: the community needs land, or a building, or a neighborhood. Co-housing developments, intentional communities on shared land, autonomous neighborhoods, religious communities on shared property — these are the physical configurations that fit. Online experiments will not produce the same result.

**Three — Founding members who already practice the principles informally.**

This is the single most important condition. The system requires people who can practice Okamanu — being exactly what they say they are. If the founders cannot do this, they will not model it for the children, and the whole transmission breaks down within a generation.

You cannot recruit people to a philosophy and expect them to embody it. You have to find people who already embody it and offer them the structure that matches how they already think. Those people exist. They are not numerous. Finding the first 30 to 50 is the hardest part of the entire implementation.

**Four — Real material foundation.**

The community needs stable food, shelter, basic resources. A community fighting daily for survival cannot run the council the way described. People need time. People who are working three jobs to feed their children do not have time to participate in a council meeting that runs for hours a day.

Either the community needs to be founded with sufficient land and resources, or it needs to develop a productive economic base early — agriculture, craft production, services — that generates enough surplus to give people the time the council requires. Twin Oaks does this through internal industries. Religious communities historically did it through farming and craft. The model is well-established.

**Five — Legal wrapper that protects the experiment.**

A cooperative, a community land trust, a religious or non-profit corporation — some legal structure that lets the community hold land and operate by its own internal rules without being legally erased by the surrounding state.

In the United States, the most flexible options are: housing cooperatives (for urban implementations), conservation land trusts combined with cooperative housing (for rural), or 501(c)(3) religious organizations (for any community willing to be legally classified as religious — and this system, with its philosophical foundation, plausibly qualifies). Similar options exist in Canada, the UK, Northern Europe, and parts of Latin America. Other jurisdictions are possible but harder.

**Six — A generation of patience.**

The system reaches its full power in the second generation. The first generation is the bridge — they came from the old systems and learned to live the new one. The second generation grows up inside it and produces the master strategists. The first generation has to commit to building something they may not personally see at full power.

This requires founding members who are not in a hurry. Anyone trying to scale fast, prove the model in five years, or harvest results quickly will subvert what makes it work.

## The Conditions That Would Make It Fail

Equally honest. If these conditions are present, the implementation will collapse — not because the system is bad, but because the conditions are not survivable.

**One — Forced implementation.**

The whole system rests on voluntary participation. If even 20% of the community is there because they have to be — because they were born into it and cannot leave, because they are economically dependent and can't afford to go, because the alternative is worse but they don't actually want this — the council corrupts. People who don't want to be there don't bring genuine voice. The work degrades. The Varonak dynamic gets traction.

The first community must be **chosen by every member**, with a real and supported option to leave at any time.

**Two — Founding in crisis.**

A community formed in panic, trauma, scarcity, or under existential threat will tend to produce strongman dynamics. The fear creates the desire for someone to take charge. Once that pattern is established, dismantling it is enormously difficult.

This system cannot be founded in crisis. It needs the calm of people who have time to think. Founding should happen during a stable period, with careful preparation, with the first few months treated as foundational rather than performative.

**Three — A Varonak in the founding circle.**

If one of the founding members is a sophisticated bad actor who understands the system and intends to use it for their own concentration of weight, and the community has not yet developed the practices to detect them, that person can shape the early culture in ways that compromise everything that comes after.

The most important screening is for the first 30 members. Look for the Okamanu pattern — people who are demonstrably the same in every room, who follow through on what they say, who can hear hard feedback without retaliation, who do not need recognition disproportionate to their contribution. These are the people who can found this. Anyone else can join later, after the practices are established and the community can hold them.

**Four — Heavy external hostility from day one.**

A community in a war zone, in active state persecution, in a refugee camp — these conditions are too unstable for the system to develop. The communities that have thrived under pressure (Christiania, Zapatistas, Rojava) all had a foundational period before the pressure intensified. They built their internal practices first, then defended them.

A first implementation in a hostile environment will fail not because the system is wrong but because no system can be built under those conditions. Start somewhere stable. Defend the model later, when the model has had time to root.

**Five — Strangers with no baseline trust.**

The first 30 to 50 members need to actually know and trust each other enough to build the initial council. Trust scales after the foundation is established — new members can join into a working community and learn it from inside. But the founding nucleus needs pre-existing relationship.

Random recruitment of strangers into a council from day one will fail. The founding nucleus should be people who have known each other for years, who have already worked together on hard things, who have already demonstrated honesty to each other under pressure.

## The Real Challenges Even When Everything Goes Right

Even with the right conditions, the implementation will face challenges. These should be expected, not feared.

**Time pressure.** The council requires hours of participation per week — sometimes per day. In a culture where people are conditioned to maximum economic productivity, finding the time is hard. The community has to organize its economic life to give itself time. This is doable but requires explicit design.

**The bad-actor problem is mitigated, not eliminated.** Even with all the practices, a sophisticated and patient bad actor can find gaps. The community will, over generations, encounter someone like this. The practices give the community the best chance of detecting and addressing it before catastrophic damage. They do not guarantee it. The community will have to become skilled at the pattern-reading work.

**Generational transmission is the hard part.** Getting the first generation to operate this way is achievable — they chose it. Getting the second generation to genuinely choose it requires that the first generation modeled it well enough that the children grew up wanting it. If the first generation performs the principles without truly living them, the children will see the gap and lose faith in the system. The transmission depends on authenticity, not instruction.

**External legal pressure.** The state will not love this system. Tax authorities, zoning regulators, child welfare agencies, building inspectors — every interface with the surrounding state is a potential point of conflict. The legal wrapper handles the routine cases. The non-routine cases require ongoing skilled engagement with external systems by community members who can speak both languages. This is real work and someone has to do it.

**Growth pressure.** A successful community attracts people who want to join. Too many too fast destroys the system. The community has to be willing to say no, often, to people who want in but are not yet right for the community or for whom the community is not yet ready. This is uncomfortable. It is also necessary.

**The first crisis.** Every community will eventually face a crisis — a death, a betrayal, a major external threat, a bad year. The first crisis is the test. If the community responds to crisis by reverting to strongman dynamics or hierarchical decision-making, the system fragments. If the community responds to crisis by deepening its commitment to the council — by trusting that even in crisis the council can find the way through — the system proves itself and becomes much harder to break going forward.

## The Realistic Implementation Path

This is what it would actually look like to start.

**Year minus three to year zero — Preparation.**

A nucleus of 5 to 15 people who already know each other and already practice the principles informally start meeting regularly. They study this design. They discuss it deeply. They identify their gaps and their honest disagreements. They walk out anyone who joins these early meetings and turns out to not be the right person for this work. By the end of three years, they have a stable nucleus of people who genuinely commit.

**Year zero — Founding.**

The nucleus secures land or shared physical space. Sets up the legal wrapper. Drafts the founding charter (we have a strong draft already — refine it for the specific community). Identifies the next 15 to 35 members and invites them in. Holds the first council meeting and does not close it.

**Years one to three — Establishment.**

The community lives the practices. They make mistakes. They name Norufiru. They face their first conflicts and their first hard external interactions. They develop the records, the rotation patterns for the Olukar, the rhythm of active sessions and recess. They start raising children inside the system — or, if they have children already, they bring those children into the council and let them participate.

**Years three to ten — Maturation.**

The practices become culture. The first elders emerge — not by appointment, but by the community recognizing whose counsel has been borne out by time. The community grows carefully, adding members at the speed of demonstrated trust. The first major crises happen and the community gets through them via the council. The children who entered young are now teenagers running logistics on real proposals.

**Years ten to thirty — Generational transmission.**

The first generation that grew up entirely inside the system reaches adulthood. They are the master strategists. They take over the central work of the council from the founders. The founders become elders — carrying the living memory, offering counsel from the longest perspective. The community has now demonstrated something no community has demonstrated before: a complete implementation of this design across one full generation, with the next generation ready to carry it.

**Years thirty and beyond — The proof.**

If the community is still functioning at this point — and on the historical evidence, it likely will be — the system is proven. Other communities can see it working. They can study it. They can adapt it to their own contexts. The model spreads not by evangelism but by demonstration.

## What Would Need to Be Built to Make This Implementable

The design is mostly complete. A few practical pieces would need to be built before someone could actually start.

**One — The First Ninety Days Handbook.** A practical guide for the founding nucleus. How to run the first council. How to set up the standing record. How to handle the first conflict. How to onboard the first new member. The current documents describe the system in mature operation; founders need a guide for the awkward early period when nothing is yet established.

**Two — Legal Wrapper Templates.** Cooperative bylaws, community land trust agreements, religious-corporation charters — tailored to this system specifically. Including the language that protects the internal governance from external legal attack. A lawyer who understands intentional communities could draft these in a few weeks.

**Three — Founding Member Selection Process.** A specific protocol for the first 30. What to look for. How to test for Okamanu. How to vet for Varonak patterns. How to handle disagreement among the founders before the formal community begins. This is more art than science but the existing literature on consensus-community founding has good material to draw from.

**Four — Land and Site Criteria.** What kind of land. What kind of jurisdiction. What kind of climate. What size. What kind of economic potential. A practical checklist for the founding nucleus to use when scouting.

**Five — The Initial Curriculum for the Children.** Not a school curriculum — a guide for parents and elders on how to bring children into the council, what to expect at each age, how to support their participation without forcing it.

These are buildable. They could be built in parallel with the first founding nucleus's preparation period. By the time a community is ready to found, the practical materials would be ready to support them.

## Said Plainly

**Yes, this system can work.**

The historical evidence is on the side of the foundation. Every component has been proven somewhere. The complete integrated design has not yet been built — but the components that are missing from each existing implementation are exactly the components this design provides.

It will not be easy. It will not produce paradise. It will require the right conditions, the right founders, the right preparation, and the patience of a generation. It will face hard challenges that the design names honestly rather than hiding.

A first community of 30 to 200 people, on shared land, in a stable jurisdiction, with a legal wrapper that protects the experiment, founded by people who already practice the principles, with a generation of patient commitment — **this community can implement this system, and within ten years can demonstrate its viability, and within thirty years can prove it.**

The first community to do this would change what humans believe is possible.

The question is not whether the system can work. The question is whether the right founders will find each other and commit to building it.

That part is not a system question. That is a people question.

The people exist. They are scattered. They have been carrying this without a name. This design gives the carrying a shape. Whether they find each other and choose to build it — that is the only thing that has not yet been determined.
