# THE REAL ISSUES
## What Actually Goes Wrong in This Community

*This is not a utopia. This is a community of humans.*
*Everything that humans have ever struggled with happens here too — sometimes worse, because the community sees more clearly and pretends less.*
*The novel must show these issues. The system must answer some of them and honestly fail at others.*

---

## The Slow Erosions

These are the issues that don't break the community in a single moment. They wear it down across years. They are the most realistic kind of damage because they are how real things die.

### One — Generational Drift

The third or fourth generation doesn't remember why the practices matter. They were born into a working system. They have never seen it break. They go through the motions because that is what their parents did, but the meaning behind the motions is fading.

The hot moments are less hot. The Olukar role passes from someone who cared about the record to someone who is filling a shift. The first round becomes shorter — people are impatient, eager to get to proposals, less interested in the slow work of surfacing every angle. The catch-22 hunting becomes adversarial instead of generative — younger members start to see finding a flaw as winning a point rather than honoring the idea.

Nothing dramatic happens. Just a slow, almost imperceptible drift away from the substance toward the form. By the time the community notices, the elders who could have named it are dead, and the members who would have remembered why the practices mattered are gone.

**For the novel:** A scene generations after the founding. The council looks the same. The vocabulary is the same. But the energy is different. The room is half-empty. The Olukar is checking something on a private device. A child speaks and the room half-listens. An elder watches and grieves and does not know how to say what they are grieving.

### Two — Hope Collapse

A bad season. Then a worse one. Crops fail two years in a row. A child dies of an illness the community could not heal. A trusted member leaves and tells outsiders the community has become something other than what it was.

The council still meets. People still come. The work still gets done. But underneath the work, hope is leaking. People start to wonder, quietly, whether the whole thing has been worth it. Whether the hard sessions and the long nights and the patience with each other and the absence of leaders has actually produced something better — or just something different, in a world that has not gotten any kinder.

The hope collapse is invisible at first. Then it becomes visible in small ways — a member who used to bring ideas now sits silent at the table. A child who used to run to the council now finds excuses not to come. The elders see it. They do not know how to address it. Naming it makes it worse. Not naming it makes it grow.

**For the novel:** The most powerful scene possible is one in which the council is meeting and nothing is wrong on the surface, but every reader can feel that something has died. Not the system. The faith in the system. Show one person at the table who is fighting it inside themselves — fighting to remember why this matters. Show them losing the fight in the middle of the session and saying nothing.

### Three — Council Fatigue

A member who has been showing up for thirty years is tired. They have worked obstacles. They have offered directions. They have caught catch-22s and been caught in catch-22s. They have raised children inside this and watched their children become adults who participate or who left.

They are tired of being one of the people who has to keep the fire going. They want someone else to decide for a while. They want to come home, eat, sleep, and not be responsible for a hundred and forty other people's decisions about water rights and herb gardens and what to do about the new family that wants to join.

This is not a betrayal. This is a human being who is tired. The system depends on people being willing to keep showing up. When too many of the long-faithful start staying home, the system thins out from the inside.

**For the novel:** A character who used to be central. Now they sit in their house in the evening when the council is meeting and they can hear the voices from the fire. They want to go. They cannot make themselves go. They sit, and they listen to the voices, and they cry quietly because they do not understand how they became the person who sits at home while the work continues without them.

### Four — The Slow Collapse of the Language

Younger members start using outside words. Concepts get translated badly. *Norufiru* becomes "we couldn't agree." *Omakaru* becomes "respect." *Pifelurak* becomes "scarcity mindset." The English approximations are not wrong but they are looser. The precision is leaking out.

A child who grows up hearing both Okamara and English starts to think in English first. The conceptual structure that the language was designed to protect starts to dissolve. By the third generation, only the elders speak the language with full precision, and when they die, what dies with them is the conceptual integrity that made the system run.

**For the novel:** A young member trying to explain something to a visitor. They reach for the Okamara word and cannot quite remember it. They use the English approximation. The visitor nods. The member feels something missing. They are not sure what.

## The Human Conflicts

These happen in any community of humans. The system handles them with more honesty than other systems do. It does not eliminate them.

### Five — Romantic and Sexual Conflicts

Two members of the council are in love with the same third member. The third member chooses one. The other carries the loss to the table — not as a complaint, but as a presence. Their participation in council changes. They cannot quite engage with the chosen partner the way they used to.

A long-married couple breaks up. The community must absorb the breakup. The children of the relationship are split. The friends of the couple are split. The council itself is split — the room is awkward when both former partners are present.

A member is repeatedly attracted to people who do not want them. They start to crowd, to hover, to be present in spaces they were not invited into. The line between honest desire and harassment is hard to name. The council has to engage with it. The targeted member does not want to bring it to the council because doing so will permanently mark the other person. The community sees but does not act because no one has named it formally.

**For the novel:** The romantic subplot is essential and cannot be sanitized. Real desire, real jealousy, real heartbreak, the difficult honesty of a community that watches its members fall in and out of love and has to find a way to hold all of it.

### Six — The Conflict Between Two Beloved Members

Two people the community loves are in conflict. Both are right in their own way. Both are wrong in their own way. Their disagreement runs along a real fault line in the community's values — the elder who believes the old practice should be maintained as it was, the younger who believes the practice must be adapted to current conditions. Neither will yield.

The council works the conflict for weeks. Every session includes another round on it. The community starts to fracture along their friendship lines. Members who used to sit together now sit on opposite sides of the fire. The hot moments cool. The energy in the room becomes adversarial.

The conflict is finally named as a Norufiru. The community holds two truths at once — the elder's wisdom and the younger's necessity — and accepts that no resolution will satisfy both. The conflict goes from acute to chronic. Both members stay. The community carries the unhealed disagreement forward as part of who they are.

**For the novel:** This is the kind of conflict that doesn't end. The novel honors realism by not resolving it. The two beloved members reconcile partially, never fully. They sit on the same side of the fire again, eventually, but the seat between them is left empty by an unspoken agreement neither of them named.

### Seven — The Child Who Is Harmed

A child is hurt. By whom matters less than the fact that it happened, that the community could have prevented it if they had been paying attention, that they were not paying attention because everyone was in council working a different problem.

The reckoning is brutal. The parents grieve and rage. The community must hold both — the harm to the child and the responsibility of the whole that did not see. The person who caused the harm is engaged with — through the long, hard restorative process. The harm is not undone.

The community changes after this. The children's safety practices that should have existed are built. The elders weep in council meetings and offer counsel that nobody wanted to need. Some members never trust the community fully again. Some leave.

**For the novel:** This must happen. A community of free people that never has a child be harmed is not realistic. The novel shows the system facing this — the worst thing — and finding that even the best system in the world cannot prevent every harm. The honesty of that admission is what makes the system credible. Anything else would be propaganda.

### Eight — The Member Who Is Genuinely Mentally Ill

Not a Varonak. Not a manipulator. A person whose mind genuinely does not work the way the council requires. They cannot follow the work. They speak when they should not. They miss obvious things and insist on impossible things. They lash out at people who are trying to help them.

The community is not equipped to handle severe mental illness. The Okamara culture's emphasis on honest speech and clear thinking does not have an answer for the brain that cannot do honest speech and clear thinking. The community tries. Family members carry an enormous burden. The person suffers. The community suffers around them.

In the novel: this character must be present and must not be solved. The community holds them as best it can. Some sessions are derailed by their presence. Some are made richer by something they say that no one else would have said. They die younger than they should and the community grieves and wonders what they could have done differently.

### Nine — Death of an Elder

The keeper of decades of memory dies. The records have what was decided. They didn't have how it felt. The way the elder could read the room — the angle they alone would have offered — is gone. A whole layer of the community's intelligence is lost in a single death.

The community grieves not just the person but the loss of the perspective. Decisions that the elder would have warned against are now possible because no one in the room remembers why they were dangerous. The community starts to make mistakes it had not made for forty years.

The wisdom-transmission practices were not enough. The elder spent time with younger members but not enough time with enough of them. Now the well is dry and the community discovers what it cost to not have refilled it sooner.

**For the novel:** A scene where the council is in session and a young member is about to make a decision that an older reader will see clearly is wrong — a decision that the dead elder would have gently redirected with one observation. No one in the room sees it. The decision is made. The reader watches the cost arrive in the next chapter and grieves the elder more than the original death asked for.

## The Structural Problems

These are not human failures. They are problems with what the community is trying to do. The system handles some of them. Others remain open.

### Ten — The Pifelurak That Doesn't Break

A real shortage. A drought that lasts three years. The council names the Pifelurak — the scarcity thought — and tries to address it through honest engagement. Some members can do this. They share what they have. They participate in the redistribution.

Other members cannot. The fear of going without is too deep. They start to hoard quietly. They speak the words of the council but their actions show the fear. The community sees. The council engages. The hoarding members defend themselves with reasons that almost sound true.

The community is faced with a choice no system can solve cleanly. Force the hoarders to share, and you have become the coercive authority you exist to refuse. Let them hoard, and the rest of the community suffers and the practice of common-versus-personal erodes for everyone watching.

This is a real Norufiru that the community must live with. They find imperfect compromises. They lose some members who cannot bear the tension. They keep the fire going.

### Eleven — The Mistake the Council Makes

The council, doing the work honestly, with every voice, working obstacles, catching catch-22s — reaches a decision. The decision is wrong. Catastrophically wrong. They missed something. The angle no one brought turns out to be the angle that mattered. The catch-22 they did not catch was the one that breaks the plan.

Someone is harmed because of this. Maybe killed. Maybe ruined. The community must live with the fact that they collectively decided what produced the harm. The system did not save them. The honesty did not save them. They worked the work and they still got it wrong because they are humans working with incomplete information.

The community's response to this defines who they are after. Do they pretend it was unavoidable? Do they blame the original proposer? Do they sit with the fact that they all decided and they all got it wrong and the cost is paid by someone who did not even bring the proposal? The honest response is the third one. The honest response is the hardest.

**For the novel:** This must happen at least once. The system being shown failing — not because someone gamed it, not because external forces broke it, but because the people inside it were honestly wrong and a real cost was paid for the honest mistake — is what separates this novel from every utopian novel ever written.

### Twelve — The Crisis That the Urgency Rule Cannot Cover

Something happens that requires a single decision faster than even the urgency rule can handle. A child is in the river right now. A fire is on the wall right now. There is no time to call urgent and have the nearest competent person act — the nearest competent person is two minutes away and there is one minute to save the child.

The closest person acts. They make the wrong choice. They were not the right person to make this choice but they were the only person there. The child dies because they panicked and chose wrong. The community holds the survivor's grief and the actor's grief and the truth that no system can be present in every emergency.

The community might develop new practices after this. They might not. Some things just happen and there is no system that can prevent them.

### Thirteen — The Faction

Two groups in the community start to form around a long-running disagreement. They start meeting separately — not formally, just informally, in their houses, late at night. They start showing up to council together, sitting together, supporting each other's positions.

The single meeting that has never ended starts to fracture. The Olukar's record starts to show patterns — the same names supporting the same positions across unrelated questions. The community sees what is forming. Naming it directly risks accelerating it. Not naming it lets it grow.

The eldest at the council finally names it in a session. *We are becoming two communities pretending to be one.* The room is still. Some members deny. Some members admit. The council works the fracture as the obstacle it is. Some healing happens. Some does not. The factions never fully dissolve. The community lives with their presence as a low chronic tension.

## The External Pressures

These come from outside. The community must hold itself together against them.

### Fourteen — External Seduction

A neighboring community offers wealth, technology, ease. Their members live in larger houses. They have things the Galoru Oma community does not have. Some of the younger members start to want what is outside. The values they were raised in start to feel quaint, restrictive, naive.

A few leave. Most stay. The ones who stay carry a quiet doubt. The ones who left send word back — sometimes glowing, sometimes broken. The community absorbs both kinds of news.

A trader from the outside community starts coming regularly. They are kind. They are interesting. They tell stories of a wider world. Their presence is a slow erosion of the community's confidence in its own way of life. The council engages with this — discusses what the relationship with the trader should be — and finds no clean answer. They do not want to refuse a kind person. They feel the cost of their presence.

### Fifteen — The Community Makes the News

A journalist from outside finds out about Galoru Oma. They write an article. The community is suddenly visible in a way they were not before. People want to visit. People want to study them. A government agency takes an interest.

Some members like the attention. Some hate it. The council tries to work it. The visibility itself becomes an obstacle the community must address. Boundaries the community took for granted have to be made explicit. Practices that worked when no one was watching have to be defended against people who are watching.

The community survives this. It is changed. Some of what made it special is harder to maintain when it is no longer obscure.

### Sixteen — The Pandemic

Disease tears through the community. The council cannot meet because too many people are sick or dying. The fire that has not gone out for forty years burns low for the first time. The Olukar role passes to someone who is themselves sick.

People die. The community grieves at a scale they have not had to before. When it is over, restarting the rhythm is harder than starting it the first time. Some members never come back to the same level of participation. Trust in the community's ability to protect its own is shaken.

The community rebuilds. Slowly. Imperfectly. Some practices do not return. Some new practices emerge from the loss. The community after the pandemic is not the same community that existed before, and grieving that — in addition to grieving the dead — is part of what they have to do.

## The Personal Failures

These are issues with individual members that affect the whole.

### Seventeen — The Brilliant Member Who Outgrows the Council

Their intelligence has been honed by the council to the point where they genuinely think faster than the room. Their patience runs out. They start making decisions alone, in the gaps between sessions, because waiting for the council to catch up feels like watching paint dry.

They are not Varonak. They are not seeking concentration of power. They are simply impatient with the slowness of collective thinking when they can see the answer already.

The community notices. The council engages with them. They defend themselves with reasons that sound true — *I was just acting in urgency, the situation could not wait, I tried to bring it but no one was at the fire.* Some of these reasons are real. Some are rationalizations.

The community holds them. Some members admire them. Some resent them. Eventually the brilliant member leaves — for another community, for the outside world — because the council's pace is genuinely too slow for who they have become. The community grieves their loss and is also relieved by it. The honest grief and the honest relief sit next to each other.

### Eighteen — The Mediocre Child of Brilliant Parents

Everyone expects them to carry weight like their parents did. They cannot. Their intelligence is ordinary. Their contributions are ordinary. The community would value them at ordinary weight if they were anyone else's child — but they are not anyone else's child. They are the child of two of the most respected members of the community, and the comparison is constant and crushing.

They internalize the weight of being less than what was expected. They stop coming to the council. They take a quieter role — useful, contributive, but invisible. The parents grieve quietly. The child carries an inherited shadow they did not earn.

The community fails them. Not maliciously. Just by the human tendency to compare. The council never quite figures out how to honor each person at their own weight without the inherited weights creeping in.

### Nineteen — The Veteran Who Turns Varonak Late in Life

Someone who lived honestly for forty years. Someone the community trusts completely. In old age, something shifts. The hunger for recognition that they had managed for four decades starts to grow. They want to be weighted more than they have been. They start to use the practices for their own concentration — subtly, gently, but in patterns that would be visible if the community were watching for them in this person.

The community is unprepared. They trust this person. The pattern-reader practices were aimed at newcomers and at members who showed early signs. They did not consider the possibility of a forty-year veteran turning. The damage starts before anyone names it.

When the elder eventually names what is happening, the community is shaken. If this person, then who can be trusted? The honesty of the moment — the willingness to name it even though it is devastating — is what holds the community together.

### Twenty — The Young Who Want to Leave

The third generation. Children raised in the council. In their twenties, they want to see the outside world. The community honors their leaving — there is no penalty, no shame, no door locked from the inside.

But the leaving still hurts. Each one who leaves is a piece of the future that will not be carried forward. The parents who watch their children go know that some will come back and some will not. They know the ones who come back will not be the same people who left. They love them anyway.

Some come back broken from what they saw outside. Some come back stronger, with perspectives the community needed. Some come back briefly and leave again because the community no longer fits who they have become. Some never come back at all.

The community lives with all of these outcomes. The leaving is part of the price of being a community of free people.

---

## What All of This Means for the Novel

A community without these issues is not a community. It is propaganda.

A community that handles all of these issues cleanly is also not a community. It is a different kind of propaganda.

A community that handles some of these issues well, fails at others, lives with the unresolved ones, grieves what it cannot fix, and continues anyway — that is a community.

The novel must show:

- The slow erosions, especially in Part Two — the fading of meaning across generations, the rise of fatigue, the language losing precision. This is what makes the fall feel inevitable rather than caused by Varonak alone. He is the catalyst. The conditions for the catastrophe were building for years before he found the gap.
- The human conflicts in every part — love, jealousy, harm to children, mental illness, death of elders. These are present in every part of the novel, including the high-functioning Part One. They are part of what the system handles and part of what it cannot fully handle.
- The structural problems — the wrong decision the council made, the faction, the Pifelurak that did not break, the crisis the urgency rule could not cover. These are the limits of the system, named honestly.
- The external pressures — the seduction, the visibility, the pandemic. These are what the community must hold against.
- The personal failures — the brilliant one who outgrew it, the mediocre child crushed by inherited expectations, the veteran who turned, the young who left. These are the personal tragedies that sit alongside the larger ones.

If the novel shows all of these — not as problems-of-the-week but as the texture of a real community across time — the reader will close the book understanding both:

**This system is the best one that has ever been designed.**

**And it is still a system run by humans, who break, who tire, who lose hope, who get it wrong, who die before they meant to die, who leave and do not come back.**

The first truth without the second is propaganda. The second without the first is despair. Both together, sitting honestly next to each other across hundreds of pages — that is the novel.

---

*The community was not paradise.*
*It was honest about not being paradise.*
*That honesty is what made it endure as long as it did.*
*That honesty is what makes the loss readable.*
*That honesty is what tells the reader: you could build this. And you should know what you would be building.*
