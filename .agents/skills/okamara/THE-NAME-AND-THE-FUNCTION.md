# THE NAME AND THE FUNCTION
## What to Call This System — And How It Actually Works

---

## Part One — The Name

There is no single existing name that captures this system completely. There are names for pieces of it. Naming the pieces honestly is the best way to show why a new name is needed.

**Council Communism** (German: Rätekommunismus). A real movement from the early 20th century — workers' councils as the basic unit of governance, no party, no state. This is the closest historical relative. It captures the council as the institution. What it does not capture: the never-ending session, the no-vote rule, the truth-grounded epistemology, the explicit answers to the bad-actor problem, the language as protection. Council communism collapsed every time it was tried because it had the institution but not the practices that make the institution survive.

**Libertarian Municipalism** (Murray Bookchin's term, also called Communalism). Direct democratic assemblies at the community level, federated upward through delegate councils. Closer in structure. Still uses votes. Still leaves the bad-actor problem unaddressed. Still relies on a philosophical commitment from participants without building the language and practices that produce that commitment generation after generation.

**Sociocracy** (also called Dynamic Governance). Consent-based decision-making, circles, double-linking. Closest in process — sociocracy is the only existing system that has worked out a real consent-based decision mechanism that is not just unanimous voting. What it does not have: the never-ending session, the truth-grounded epistemology, the elder weighting, the restorative justice approach, the philosophical foundation. Sociocracy was designed for organizations and adapted to communities. This system was designed for communities from the start.

**Anarcho-communism**. The political philosophy. Has the diagnosis and the principle. Does not have the design. Anarcho-communist communities have existed and have mostly collapsed because the design was incomplete.

**Consensus Democracy**. The procedural label for unanimous-agreement decision-making. Captures the no-vote feature but not much else. Often degrades into either decision paralysis or quiet majority dominance disguised as consensus.

None of these names fits. Each captures one face of what we have built.

---

### What to Call It

The honest answer: **this system needs a new name, because what it is has not fully existed before.**

Three candidates, in three registers:

**In Okamara — the language of the system itself:**
**Omakaruvu.** The council institution. The word that already names what the system is built around. When the people of Galoru Oma describe how they govern themselves, they do not use a separate word for the political system. They name the council. The council is the system. *Omakaruvu* — "the great forging place" in literal translation, "the council" in functional translation — is the name in the language designed to hold the concepts precisely.

**In plain English — for outside transmission:**
**Continuous Council Governance**, or in shorthand, **Council Continuism**.
- *Council* — the institution (no leaders, no votes, every voice).
- *Continuous* — the never-ending session (the same first meeting that has never ended).
- *Governance* — what it actually is, said directly.

This name is functional. A person who hears it for the first time can derive most of the system from the words themselves: a council that never closes. The mechanics flow from that.

**In philosophical register — for the deeper conversation:**
**Onakaoru Continuism.** *Onakaoru* — the sovereign self. The individual whose sovereignty is prior to the council and cannot be forged away by it. *Continuism* — the never-ending engagement with the work. This name captures what most political philosophers will want to know first: where the individual stands in relation to the collective. The answer is in the name. The individual stands prior. The collective is continuous. Neither overrides the other.

**Recommended primary name: Council Continuism** (or its full form, Continuous Council Governance). Use *Omakaruvu* when speaking inside the framework. Use *Onakaoru Continuism* when defending the framework against the charge that it dissolves the individual into the collective — because that name shows, in two words, that it does the opposite.

---

### Why a New Name Matters

Names carry inheritance. If you call this system "anarchism," people import everything they think anarchism is — including all the failures of every previous attempt and all the strawmen built up by its opponents over two centuries. If you call it "communism," same problem, even worse. If you call it "democracy," people assume voting and majority rule.

A new name lets the system be understood on its own terms. *Council Continuism* is unfamiliar enough that a person hearing it has to actually ask what it means, rather than assuming they already know.

Unfamiliarity is, in this case, a feature.

## Part Two — How It Functions

What follows is the practical mechanics. How a real day in this community looks. How a decision actually gets made. How a problem gets raised. How a person joins or leaves. Not the philosophy. The function.

---

### The Council Meeting in Daily Practice

The council meeting is always in session. In practice, this means:

**Active sessions.** The community gathers at agreed times — in most communities, daily for a working session of two to four hours, often in the morning before the day's work or in the evening after it. The session is open to every member. Attendance is not mandatory in any formal sense — but the record will show who was present when what was decided, and a person who never attends will find their voice carries less weight by simple absence, not by penalty.

**Recess.** Between active sessions, the meeting is in recess. Not closed. The agenda persists. Open problems wait, documented in the record, for the next active session. People carry the work in their minds during recess — the best resolutions to hard problems often surface in this space, when the formal pressure of the council table is lifted.

**The standing record.** A living document — written, kept by a rotating role called the *Olukar* (literally "the one who holds the open"), updated continuously. The record contains: every open problem with its history, every resolved problem with its path, every Norufiru with its full account, every decision made and what it cost to reach. The record is the community's shared memory. New members are introduced to the community by being introduced to the record.

---

### How a Decision Gets Made — Step by Step

A community member sees a situation that needs collective attention. Could be anything: a question about the use of common land, a proposed change to how something is done, a conflict between two members, a new opportunity, an outside relationship to evaluate.

**Step 1 — Bring it to the council.** No permission needed. The meeting is open. The person brings the matter to the next active session, or — if it cannot wait — calls for an emergent session through the urgency mechanism (more on that below). They state what they have brought, plainly. The Olukar records it as an open item.

**Step 2 — The first round: every voice.** The council goes around. Every person at the table speaks to the matter. Not "what do you think we should do" — that comes later. First: *what do you see?* Each person describes the situation as they observe it, what concerns them about it, what angles they bring to it. The first round is purely for surfacing the full picture. No one decides anything yet. No one even proposes anything yet. Everyone listens.

**Step 3 — Naming obstacles.** After the first round, the council names obstacles. An obstacle is something that prevents a clean resolution: a constraint, a competing interest, a missing piece of information, a value that pulls in a different direction. Every obstacle gets named explicitly and added to the record. The obstacles are the real problem. The original question is rarely the real problem.

**Step 4 — Working obstacles.** The council takes obstacles one at a time. For each obstacle, the question is: *what direction would resolve this?* Not "what is the answer," but "what direction." Members offer directions. Each direction is engaged with: what does it cost, who does it affect, does it create new obstacles, does it actually move past the original obstacle? Directions that hold are kept. Directions that fail are recorded — *we tried this and it did not move past the obstacle because* — so that the same direction is not retried later without new information.

**Step 5 — Convergence or Norufiru.** As obstacles are worked, one of two things happens. Either the directions converge — the council finds a resolution that addresses every named obstacle and that no member opposes from a substantive ground — or they do not. If they converge, the resolution is named, the Olukar records it, the council moves on. If they do not converge — if there is a real Norufiru, a wall that no direction can pass without forcing something the community does not want to force — the Norufiru is named honestly. The record receives it. The community does not pretend a resolution exists when it does not. *We met this and we did not find a way through. Here is what we tried. Here is why each direction failed. Come back to this only with something we did not have.*

**Step 6 — The resolution holds.** A resolution that came out of this process holds because the people who built it are the people who will live with it. There are no losers waiting to undo it. There is no minority that was overruled. Every voice was at the formation. Every obstacle was named and worked. The decision is genuinely the community's, not a faction's, not a leader's, not a majority's.

### How Urgency Is Handled

Sometimes a situation cannot wait for an active session. A child is hurt. A storm is coming. An immediate threat is at the edge of the community. The system has to handle this without creating permanent authority.

**The urgency rule.** Any member can call urgent. Three things happen immediately:

**One — The nearest cluster acts.** Whoever is present and competent acts. A medical emergency is handled by whoever can handle it. A storm is responded to by whoever is at hand. The action happens without waiting for council.

**Two — The action is bounded.** The urgent actor takes the minimum action necessary to address the urgency. Not more. They are not making policy. They are not setting precedent. They are responding to *this* situation, *right now*. The boundary is part of the rule.

**Three — The action is brought to the next active session.** The urgent actor reports what they did, why, and what they observed. The council reviews — not to punish (the action was already taken in good faith) but to learn. Was the action right? Should the principles that guided it be encoded for next time? Is there a better practice the community wants to adopt? The urgent action becomes a teaching moment, recorded, integrated.

**The urgency mechanism does not create a class of urgent actors.** Anyone can call urgent. Anyone can act in urgency. The competence to act in a specific urgency comes from the specific person's specific knowledge and presence — not from a position. There is no "emergency council." There is only the community's standing capacity to respond to what is in front of it.

### How a Conflict Between Members Is Handled

Two members are in conflict. Could be anything: a dispute over the use of a tool, a disagreement about how to raise a child they share, a hurt that one feels the other caused.

**Step 1 — Direct.** The members are expected first to attempt direct resolution. The community's culture supports this — speaking the difficult thing directly is the first practice of Okamanu. Most conflicts resolve here.

**Step 2 — A small council.** If direct resolution fails, the members bring it to a small council — three to five people they both trust. Not a tribunal. Not judges. Witnesses and helpers. The small council holds space while the two members each speak fully, hears them, and helps them find what neither could find alone.

**Step 3 — The full council.** If the small council cannot help — or if the conflict has implications for the whole community — it goes to the full council. The same process: every voice, named obstacles, worked directions. The conflict is treated as a council matter because it is.

**Step 4 — Restoration, not punishment.** If a real harm was done — one member genuinely wronged the other — the council works toward restoration. What was damaged? What would repair look like? What does the harmed person need? What does the person who caused harm need to understand? The goal is not to make the wrongdoer suffer in proportion to the harm. The goal is to repair what was damaged and prevent recurrence.

**Step 5 — In serious cases, withdrawal.** If the harm was severe and the person who caused it cannot or will not engage in restoration — or if the pattern of harm continues despite restoration efforts — the community withdraws trust and, ultimately, membership. Not as punishment. As honest acknowledgment that this person and this community are no longer compatible. The person leaves with whatever is genuinely theirs. The record holds what happened. The community continues.

This is the hardest case. The system does not pretend it is easy. It is harder than calling the police and outsourcing the problem to the state. It is also more honest, more proportionate, and more likely to actually repair what was damaged rather than just adding suffering on top of suffering.

### How a Person Joins

**Visiting.** A person arrives. They are welcomed. They observe. They participate in daily life — meals, work, the council meeting. They are not yet a member. They are visiting. The visit can last weeks or months. There is no pressure to decide.

**Signaling intention.** When the visitor wishes to seek membership, they say so. The community engages with the request — not as a vote, not as an evaluation, but as a council matter. What does this person bring? What do they need? What are the obstacles, on either side?

**The threshold.** Membership is not granted by acclamation or denied by a single objection. It is worked, in council, until either the membership holds (the community sees the person and the person sees the community, and both are willing) or a Norufiru is named (this person and this community are not a good match, with no judgment of either, and the person is helped on their way to a place that fits better).

**Provisional period.** A new member begins with full voice but light weight. Their voice is heard fully — every voice always is. Their counsel carries the weight of someone who has not yet demonstrated themselves over time. Weight builds with demonstrated life. Some members carry significant weight after a few years; some never carry more than the baseline. Weight is observed, not granted.

**Departure.** Any member can leave at any time. There is no penalty for leaving. The person leaves with what is genuinely theirs. The record notes the departure. The community continues. The door is not locked from the inside — and a community that locks its doors from the inside has stopped being a community of free people.

### How Resources Are Held and Used

**The common.** The land, the water, the shared infrastructure, the tools that serve everyone, the knowledge that belongs to no one in particular — these are held by the community. Their use is a council matter. The Olukar maintains the record of what is held in common and what its current use is.

**The personal.** What a person has made with their hands, what they have brought from outside the community as theirs, what they use in their daily life — this is theirs. The community's culture distinguishes clearly between common and personal, and the distinction is respected as a matter of practice.

**Surplus.** When the community produces more than its members need, the surplus is a council question. Not a market question (highest bidder). Not a redistribution question (everyone gets equal regardless). A council question: what does the community need most? What serves the whole? Common surplus stays with the community to be deployed where the council finds it best deployed. Personal surplus belongs to the person who produced it, who may use it, share it, or contribute it to the common as they choose.

**Scarcity.** When the community faces scarcity — drought, crop failure, external constraint — the Pifelurak (the scarcity thought) is named explicitly. The council works the scarcity as the obstacle it is. The community shares what is available. No one starves while another hoards. This is not enforced by rule. It is held by the community's practice and by the visibility of every member to every other member.

### How the Outside World Is Handled

The community exists in a world full of states, corporations, laws, money, and other communities that operate on different principles. The interface is real and must be handled.

**The legal wrapper.** The community organizes itself, in the outside world's terms, as a legal entity — most commonly a cooperative, a trust, or a similar structure that allows it to hold land, enter into agreements, and interface with external systems. The legal wrapper is the community's external face. Internally, the community does not operate by the wrapper's rules. The wrapper exists so that the community can operate by its own rules without being legally erased by a world that does not yet recognize what it is.

**External relationships.** Trade with other communities, agreements with neighbors, interactions with institutions — all of these are council matters. The community decides, in council, what relationships to enter and on what terms. The legal wrapper executes what the council decides.

**Defense.** A community of free people in a world that includes coercive force has to think about defense. This system has no army, no police, no internal force. It has the council's collective intelligence, the community's relationships with neighboring communities, and the community's location and design choices. The system does not pretend to have a complete answer to violence. It has the most defensible position a non-coercive community can have, and it relies on the attractiveness of its alternative as its long-term protection.

### How Knowledge Is Transmitted

**Children.** Children are present in the council meeting from when they can attend. They hear the work. They speak when they have something to say — and the room goes quiet to hear them, because the language they bring is the language of someone who has not yet learned to lie. As they grow, they learn the practices by being inside them. Formal education for skills that require systematic instruction (reading, mathematics, technical knowledge) is organized as a common resource — available to every child, not as a credential system but as a capability-building one.

**Elders.** Elders carry the living memory of the council. The record holds what was decided. The elder holds how it felt, what the room was like, what the obstacle looked like before it was named. This memory is transmitted by the elder spending time with younger members, telling the stories, walking the land that the decisions concerned, sitting in the council and offering the angles that only their long view can offer.

**The land.** Knowledge of the specific place the community lives — its water, its soil, its seasons, its patterns — is transmitted by living with it. By working it. By spending time on it with someone who knows it and being shown what they see. This knowledge cannot be transmitted any other way. A community that loses its land knowledge has lost its foundation.

### How the System Maintains Itself Over Generations

**The language.** Okamara, as a complete language, is the carrier of the system's concepts. A child raised speaking it cannot easily think the thoughts that would dissolve the system, because the language does not provide fluent grammar for them. A community that maintains the language maintains the conceptual integrity of the system.

**The practice.** The Okamanu principle — being exactly what you say you are, in every room — is the daily practice. It is taught by being modeled. A community where the elders demonstrably practice it produces children who learn it before they can articulate it.

**The record.** The standing record carries the history. New members are introduced to the community through the record. The record holds what was tried, what worked, what failed, what was named as Norufiru. The record prevents the community from repeating its own mistakes.

**The honest review.** Periodically — most communities choose a long cycle, often once every several years — the council reviews the system itself. Not a vote on whether to continue it. A council meeting on whether the system is still serving the community, where the gaps are, what needs to be revised. The system is not held above examination. It is held to its own standard: does it work? Where does it fail? What is the honest direction forward?

---

## Said Plainly

**The name is Council Continuism.** A new name for a system that is genuinely new — assembled from correct pieces of every previous attempt, but new as a complete design.

**The function is the council meeting that never ends.** The community gathers, works what is in front of it, names what cannot be resolved, recesses to live, reconvenes to continue. Every decision is built by the people who will live with it. Every voice is at the formation. No votes. No leaders. No representatives. Every harm is met with restoration rather than punishment. Every resource is held as common or personal with the distinction maintained by practice. Every new member joins an ongoing conversation. Every elder carries the living memory. Every problem is the work.

This is how the community of Galoru Oma functioned in the novel.

This is how a real community can function now.

The same design, the same name, the same way it works.

The fiction shows it working and breaking. The design shows how to build it so it works and does not break.

---

*The name is Council Continuism.*
*The function is the council that never ends.*
*The work is the same work, in every generation, forever.*
